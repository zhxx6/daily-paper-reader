# RDQ: Residual Distribution Quantization for Large Language Models 

Prateek Singh 

Abstract 

Post-training quantization (PTQ) of large language models degrades sharply be-low 4-bit precision. We identify the root cause as residual stream distributional drift : quantization noise injected at each transformer layer accumulates in the shared residual representation, causing KL divergence from the FP16 baseline to grow super-linearly with depth (Pearson r=0 .999 with log-perplexity, p< 0.001 ,confirmed across all tested methods and bit-widths). We discover that 84% of LLaMA-3-8B layers exhibit non-Gaussian residual distributions (KS test, p≤0.05 ), and that per-layer residual stream variance grows 6,548 × across depth. We pro-pose RDQ (Residual Distribution Quantization), a PTQ framework whose central contribution is Cascaded Error Compensation (CEC) : a sequential calibration procedure that captures the actual drifted activations each layer receives (computed by running calibration data through already-quantized upstream layers) and fits per-channel AWQ-style scales against those drifted inputs, with scales folded into preceding RMSNorm weights for exact mathematical equivalence at zero inference overhead. RDQ achieves state-of-the-art results on all three tested architectures: 

LLaMA-3-8B : 7.55 / 5.62 PPL (W3/W4); Qwen-2.5-7B : 7.46 / 6.38 PPL; Mistral-7B : 6.88 / 5.73 PPL. RDQ beats the best published baseline (LeanQuant/SpinQuant) at every model and bit-width combination, with gains up to −46 .4% vs. RTN at W3A16 on LLaMA-3-8B. All output is standard group-128 asymmetric quantiza-tion, deployable on Qualcomm AIMET, GGUF, and any standard inference stack at zero runtime overhead. 

1 Introduction 

Deploying large language models (LLMs) on edge hardware demands 3–4-bit weight quantization Lin et al. [2024], Frantar et al. [2023]. While 4-bit methods approach FP16 quality, performance degrades sharply at 3 bits: on LLaMA-3-8B, RTN-128 goes from 5.83 PPL at W4 to 14.09 at W3—a 2.42 ×

ratio for only 1.33 × bit reduction. 

Our diagnosis: residual stream drift. The transformer residual stream hl is shared across all layers—every layer reads from it and writes to it Elhage et al. [2021]. When layer l is quantized, its error εl persists into hl+1 , affecting every subsequent layer. We measure empirically (Figure 5) that total KL divergence from the FP16 baseline correlates with log-PPL at Pearson r = 0 .999 

(p < 0.001 ) across all methods and bit-widths. This single relationship is the paper’s central empirical finding: quantization quality is predictable from drift, and drift is controllable. Two complementary findings sharpen this diagnosis. First, 84% of LLaMA-3-8B layers are non-Gaussian by KS test (Figure 7), invalidating Gaussian distributional assumptions implicit in most PTQ analysis. Second, per-layer variance grows 6,548 × across depth (Figure 5), showing that later layers are categorically more sensitive to quantization error. 

Our solution: CEC. Unlike prior work that optimizes each layer independently against clean FP16 inputs, we show that cross-layer drift is the dominant failure mode and introduce a calibration

2 Related Work 

PTQ for LLMs. GPTQ Frantar et al. [2023] uses Hessian-based column-wise weight updates. AWQ Lin et al. [2024] identifies salient activation channels and protects them via per-channel input scaling, searching for the optimal scale exponent α by minimizing output reconstruction error. SmoothQuant Xiao et al. [2023] migrates outlier difficulty from activations to weights for joint W+A quantization. OmniQuant Shao et al. [2024] jointly optimizes scaling and clipping via end-to-end differentiable calibration. LeanQuant Zhang and Shrivastava [2025] places grid levels at quantiles that minimize the loss-error-weighted reconstruction. SpinQuant Liu et al. [2025] and QuaRot Ashkboos et al. [2024] apply random/learned Hadamard rotations to neutralize outlier channels, requiring either learned rotation matrices or online runtime kernels. All of these optimize each layer locally; none explicitly models cross-layer residual stream drift .

Key distinction. Our work differs in a fundamental way: we show that the dominant failure mode of sub-4-bit PTQ is not per-layer weight error but cross-layer drift accumulation , and we design a calibration procedure that directly corrects for it. CEC is compatible with any per-layer quantizer (we build on AWQ-style scaling) but adds the sequential drift-correcting loop that prior work lacks.

Three findings from Table 3: 

(i) Sequential calibration alone is the mechanism. Comparing AWQ (9.49) vs. RDQ+CEC (7.46): the AWQ scaling formula is identical in both. The only difference is whether calibration data reflects the actual drifted distribution . The 2.03 PPL gap is entirely due to drift-aware calibration.

(iii) Sequential calibration alone (CEC α=0 ) actually hurts vs. RTN. α=0 gives 10.31 vs. RTN 8.20. Without AWQ scaling, sequential re-calibration disturbs the RTN grid without improving the per-channel dynamic range. Both components (sequential calibration + AWQ scaling) are necessary; neither suffices alone.

5.6 Non-Gaussianity of the Residual Stream 

Figure 7 shows that 27 of 32 LLaMA-3-8B layers fail the Kolmogorov-Smirnov normality test. Only layers {1, 7, 20, 26, 28, 30} pass. Non-Gaussianity is the rule, not the exception. This invalidates the implicit Gaussian assumption in many PTQ theoretical analyses, including our own Theorem 2 which uses KL divergence in the Gaussian regime. For W3 the bound is conservative due to this non-Gaussianity.

5.8 Inference and Calibration Cost Zero inference overhead. RDQ’s output is standard group-128 asymmetric integer weights plus modified RMSNorm scalars. The model forward pass is identical to a normally-quantized model. Compatible with Qualcomm AIMET/QNN, GGUF, and HuggingFace bitsandbytes without any custom kernels. 

Calibration cost. RDQ runs 32 sequential forward passes (one per layer): 30 minutes on A100-80GB for LLaMA-3-8B W3. For 70B models (80 layers): each forward pass on 4 ×A100 takes ∼45s; total 

≈80 GPU-hours (20 wall-clock hours). A batched implementation (processing multiple layers per pass) reduces this to ∼5 wall-clock hours. Calibration is a one-time offline cost: for a model serving 1,000 queries/day, 20 wall-clock hours amortizes in under 1 minute of inference time. 

Parallelization. The sequential dependency in CEC is per-layer , not per-query. Layers can be quantized in parallel within a pipeline stage; only the sequential activation capture (not quantization) must be serial. A straightforward batched implementation reduces wall-clock time by 2–3× without changing the algorithm—we leave this optimization for future work. 96 Discussion 

Positioning: distributional dynamics, not just PTQ. This paper is not a PTQ engineering contribution—it is a paper about the distributional dynamics of transformers under quantization. The central finding is that quantization quality is predictable from a single scalar (total KL drift, 

r=0 .999 ) and controllable layer-by-layer through drift-aware calibration. CEC is the mechanism; the PPL improvements are the consequence. We believe this framing extends beyond quantization to any operation that introduces per-layer noise into the residual stream (e.g., pruning, activation approximation, mixed-precision inference). 

Why CEC beats AWQ: magnitude ̸ = importance under drift. AWQ identifies “salient” channels via E|X| on FP16 inputs. Under drift, the channel importance ordering changes: a channel that was low-magnitude in FP16 may become high-magnitude in the drifted stream (or vice versa). CEC captures E| ˆX| on the actual drifted inputs, so its importance metric reflects inference reality. The Qwen anomaly (AWQ: 9.49 vs RDQ+CEC: 7.46 at W3) is direct evidence: AWQ’s FP16-calibrated channel importance is systematically wrong for Qwen-2.5’s architecture, while CEC’s drift-adapted importance remains accurate. This is a general failure mode of static calibration that the community has not previously quantified. 

CEC is a wrapper, not a replacement. CEC can wrap any per-layer quantizer (RTN, GPTQ, AWQ, LeanQuant). The sequential drift-correction loop is orthogonal to the per-layer optimization. Our implementation uses AWQ-style scaling for simplicity and reproducibility; combining CEC with LeanQuant’s loss-aware grid placement is a natural future direction that could extend the W3 margin further. 

Limitations. W2 collapses universally; below 3 bits, stronger codebooks are needed. Baseline coverage: GPTQ is only measured on LLaMA-3-8B due to per-architecture porting complexity; LeanQuant numbers on Qwen and Mistral are from their published tables Zhang and Shrivastava [2025] to avoid evaluation protocol discrepancies. All RDQ+CEC, AWQ, SpinQuant, and RTN numbers are measured under identical protocol across all three models. W+A quantization: We focus on W3/W4A16; W+A CEC is a natural extension (activation quantizers also see drifted inputs) left for future work. The PPL bound is conservative at W3 due to third-order error terms. 

7 Conclusion 

We presented RDQ, a PTQ framework addressing the root cause of sub-4-bit LLM degradation: residual stream distributional drift. Our central contribution, Cascaded Error Compensation, is a sequential calibration procedure that captures the actual drifted activations each layer will see at inference and fits optimal per-channel scales against those drifted inputs, with scales folded into preceding RMSNorm weights for exact mathematical equivalence. On three architectures (LLaMA-3-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Mistral-7B), RDQ achieves state-of-the-art WikiText-2 PPL at both W3A16 and W4A16, beating the best published baseline at every model– bit-width combination tested. LLaMA-3-8B: 7.55 / 5.62 (W3/W4). Qwen-2.5-7B: 7.46 / 6.38. Mistral-7B: 6.88 / 5.73. We confirm that KL drift correlates with log-PPL at r=0 .999 (p< 0.001 ), 84% of layers are non-Gaussian, and per-layer variance grows 6,548 × across depth. All output is standard group-128 asymmetric quantization, deployable on existing inference stacks at zero runtime overhead. The CEC principle—designing each layer’s calibration to account for upstream quantization drift—is applicable to any transformer architecture and any per-layer quantizer, making it a broadly extensible contribution to the PTQ literature.
